The Revolt of "Mother"

Mary Wilkins Freeman

Mary Wilkins Freeman is a local colorist and an early realist. She has a deep understanding of how her characters feel and knows the struggle of her female characters. she was born on October 31, 1852, in Randolph, Massachusetts. She then died March 13, 1930, in Metuchen, New Jersey. Mary was an American writer known for her stories and novels of frustrated lives in villages. After studying for at Mount Holyoke Remale Seminary in 1870-1871, she went to live with her parents in Brattleboro Vermont, and continued to read on her own. After, she started writing children's stories. Once her parents haad past, she moved back to Randolph, and within that year published her first adult story.



In 1902, Mary Wilkins Freeman married Charles M. Freeman who was from Metuchen, New Jersey and proceeded to move there to be with him until she passed. Her short stories were based on various ways people react with poverty and restrictive circumstances. She uses countryside settings, and New England villiages that help with the local colour movement. However, Mary avoided sentimentality within her pieces. She produced a dozen volumes of short stories along with several novels. Mary Wilkins Freeman is heavily remembered for the first two collections of stories, A Humble Romance and Other Stories (1887) and A New England Nun and Other Stories (1891, and the novel Pembroke (1894).
